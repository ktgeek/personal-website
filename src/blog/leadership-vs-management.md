## Preface

One of the great gifts of where I was working in the 2015-2019 timeframe was getting lots of leadership training. We
routinely did in person leadership training, as well as checking in. A person I look back on as a mentor liked to open
every meeting with a "leadership thought" which was a 5-10 minute presentation/story/reflection on some aspect of
leadership. (This is definitely something I've stolen and used since then.) I had some natural ability before this, but
the repeated and focused thinking about it really helped shape me and increase my abilities. Consider this post a
leadership thought...

## Leadership Thought

I've found myself saying on multiple occasions throughout my career... "We used to be led, but now we are
just managed." The people I was commiserating with at those times nodded knowingly. I often think about what I
really meant by that.

If I had to boil it down to two bullet points it would be:

* Management is telling people what to do
* Leadership is getting people to follow you and accomplish tasks

Maybe when I mean "Management" here, I'm talking about its lowest, most transactional form. Any leadership role has a
touch of management to it. As I learned when I took LPMA[^1] you sometimes need to employ different leadership styles
that may lean more into prescriptive management depending on projects and where the team you're leading are at.

But as I start to think down these two paths, many core differences jump out at me.

The first one is trust and relationships. Leadership invests time in getting to know the people they are leading;
understanding their strengths, weaknesses, and passions to help them find the best way to contribute and to grow as
people. That investment in time and that "getting to know each other" builds trust. As explored in Stephen Covey's book
"The Speed of Trust" that trust is where speed and efficiency come in. I'd argue that's also where innovation comes in,
trust allows people to be comfortable taking risks and potentially failing. Management, in its worst form, is just
concerned with tasks getting done and views people as interchangeable cogs.

Along those lines, Management gives you subtasks to tackle. They don't do a good job of "sharing the why" or providing
larger context than "do this because I said." Without the why or context, you may end up filling the letter of the task
without hitting the target that the business was shooting for. Having the why shared with you is an action that brings
you into the team. You want to do well and succeed, not just for yourself, but for the greater good you now understand
your part in.

Also, by hoarding knowledge (knowingly or not) in this way, they are telegraphing that they are not open to alternative
ideas and only want things done as prescribed. Leadership, on the other hand, cares less about how things are done
(barring any regulatory requirements, etc) and more open to others' ideas. I know there have been times when I've talked
with my team and said something like "We need to get X done, so we should execute by doing Y." With the trust I had with
my team they knew I was using the suggestion as the start of a conversation and often suggested a Z way of doing things
that turned out for the better.

There's more I could say on this, but I'll save those for later posts. For now I'll leave you with a closing story.

## Closing Story

As a mentor of other leaders, I had a very proud moment this past weekend on a campout with my scout troop. Our Senior
Patrol Leader (SPL)[^2] was working with his patrol leaders to cleanup camp on Sunday morning. After laying out what
needed to be accomplished in terms of breaking down some canopies, the patrol leaders started to take off. Immediately,
the SPL called them back and said "hold on, I want to tell you the why so you can proceed without checking in with me if
I'm busy and you have a question."

Having known and been around our SPL since he joined the troop and having watched him grow, this was a very proud moment
for me. It was clear he's starting to grow from management into leadership. I wish I could take all the credit, but he's
also taken [National Youth Leadership Training](https://www.scouting.org/programs/scouts-bsa/resources/nylt/) which is
probably what should get the most credit.

[^2]: Scouting America Scouts BSA troops are scout-led, meaning the youth are in charge and organizing and getting
