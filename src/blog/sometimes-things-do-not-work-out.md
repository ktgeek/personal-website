Sometimes, despite everyone's best efforts, things just don't work out. A recent change in my life has me thinking a lot
about this.

As a kid, we start to learn this through trying out sports and clubs. Maybe we're not good at a particular sport or
whole family of them for that matter. Maybe we found out we like team sports like basketball instead of more solo sports
like swimming. Maybe we thought we were into anime until we joined the anime club and met other kids REALLY into anime
and didn't fit in. Maybe we like reading the yearbook more than helping create it. There's all sorts of stumbles along
the way.

As a teenager, we REALLY learn this in dating. The first hurdle is just asking someone out, which we all know doesn't
always end positively. If you do happen to get a yes sometimes after the third or fourth date, despite each person
wanting to go on the date and continuing to connect sometimes it's just the wrong mix of personalities. (Heck, sometimes
that's months or even years later.) Often folks think they have to try harder at this stage, but after a few
relationships you learn that it's ok if it doesn't work out as long as we follow the campsite[^1] rule.

We tend to forget these lessons in the working world. When confronted with things not being a good fit, for whatever
reason, we assume the problem is us and that we have to work harder to fit into the job. Part of the reason, unlike
dating or sports, is you have a job to earn money so you can live your life. It's a really difficult decision especially
on the monetary side, but that's the problem: we tend to focus only on the monetary aspects of the decision. When you
try to force a bad fit the effects can be dramatic on one's mental and emotional well being[^2].

I recently left a company after a very short duration, the shortest of my professional career. I tried really hard to
make it work, my direct manager and peer both did as well. The teams I worked directly with were great. But the overall
company and I were not a match for a variety of reasons. Despite everyone's good faith in the interview process and
working together, there were some aspects of the company and my core values that just didn't match that I couldn't get
past. It was leaking out of work and affecting my mood and mental health for my family. (So much so a few days after I
decided to leave my wife said to me "You're back! You haven't been here for months.")

In thinking about how hard it was for me to make the choice to leave, I realize I just forgot the lesson of things not
fitting and not working out. It wasn't giving up. It wasn't failing. It was recognizing a lesson I learned over and over
again outside of work... things don't work out sometimes, it's no one's fault necessarily, it just wasn't a fit.

And so, now, much like when I was in the dating world... time to ask a bunch of ~~people~~ companies out and see what
fits. (Oh, if you're hiring, checkout my [resume](/resume).)

[^1]: The campsite rule is "leave the campsite better than you found it." In dating, it's the same thing, but "other
    person" instead of campsite.

[^2]: As an aside... Those of us in Gen X were taught to suck it up and accept toxic workplaces and ignore work-life
    balance lest one get laid off. I also recently developed a theory that most people haven't worked in happy and
    healthy environments, so they don't know they can expect that. Worse, they don't know they can and should build that
    for other people. From 2015 to about 2022 or so, I lived in that place. It doesn't mean there weren't challenges to
    solve or hard times to go through, but I worked in an environment I will forever try to recreate.
