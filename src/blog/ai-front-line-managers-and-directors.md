I answered this first biking question, but wanted to take some time to think about my answer to this second one.

In my career I've held the roles of Director of Engineering, Engineering Manager, and various individual
contributor roles. During my time as an EM and as a Director, I really had a chance to deepen my understanding of
leadership and what those types of roles should be doing. When I see the industry trend that Josh points out, all I can
see is a company aiming a gun right at its foot.

When I became a director, my mentor (and then boss) told me "When you were an EM or an IC you were working *in* the
business. Now that you're a director, you work *on* the business." It took me a fair amount of time (and growth as a
leader) to understand what that meant. Where I eventually settled is that as a director, my job is to work on the engine
that is (or drives) the company. An EM or IC works on steering the car or loading and unloading cargo, but they can't
make subtle changes that make the car go faster or work more efficiently. Where the metaphor falls down is that the
director is also responsible for whether the people steering are the right people for the job — coaching them, or
making more drastic changes if needed.

The other thing that is important as a good leader is that a large amount of your time is taken up by things that AI
can't accelerate for you. One glaring example is one-on-ones. They are a very important function of a director. That
takes time that AI can't remove from your plate... as I've talked about in a [previous
post](/blog/leadership-vs-management), building relationships and in turn trust is a key part of leadership and a key
part of understanding the ways in which you can support and accelerate the team. Reflecting and strategizing after
one-on-ones is maybe where AI can help you refine thoughts, but doesn't sort it all.

The last thing that comes to mind is the ideal patrol...er... team size. (Yes, I get paid by the scouting references I
drop.) All the way back in 1919, in his Aids to Scoutmastership[^1], Lord Baden-Powell wrote that the ideal patrol size
is 6-8 scouts. In my mind, this is the ideal team size for software engineers as well, for many of the same reasons.
The team is small enough that everyone, but especially the leader, can know each other and work together, but large
enough to have a diversity of skills and perspectives. LBP also noted by observation, that this is the size at which a
leader can lead "by consent" rather than top down force.

As you move up the chain from EM to director, you have to not only be able to lead your direct reports, but also have
skip-level relationships and one-on-ones with the EM's reports. My experience is that the ideal size of direct reports
for a director is about 4 to 5 EMs, given the skip-level responsibilities and to leave time to work *on* the
business as discussed above.

So the more you remove that support structure from under a director, and the more you put on their plate, the less
effective your business will be over time. More importantly the more direct reports you give a director, the less they
can support those who report to them overall. I don't see any other long-term consequence of the current trend. Of
course, I made the bold assumption that one cares about the long term... but that's a discussion for another time.

[^1]: "The patrol system is not one method in which Scouting can be carried on. It is the only method." -- Lord
